\chapter{Release~2: Transactions, Intelligence \& Delivery}
\label{chap:release2}

\section*{Introduction}

This chapter presents the second release, \textit{Transactions,
Intelligence \& Delivery}, which turns the populated marketplace of
Release~1 into a transacting, self-moderating and deployable product. It
opens with a release overview --- a release-scoped use-case diagram and
the subset of the domain model touched by the release --- followed by
the release backlog. It then presents the two sprints of the release at
the same depth as Chapter~\ref{chap:release1}. \textbf{Sprint~3 (Orders,
Payment \& Administration)} delivers the cart, the multi-store checkout,
the Stripe payment flow and the administration tooling; \textbf{Sprint~4
(AI Verification \& Premium)} delivers the automated AI pipeline, the
premium tier, and --- folded into the same sprint --- the testing and
the DevOps and deployment that bring the platform to production. The
present chapter details Sprint~3 first.

\section{Release Overview}

Release~2 completes the marketplace over two sprints. Sprint~3 makes the
catalogue transactable: customers fill a cart, check out across multiple
stores in a single order, and pay through Stripe, while administrators
moderate stores and products, curate featured content and manage
categories. Sprint~4 adds the intelligence and delivery layers: automated
AI verification of new stores and products, the AI database-chat
assistant, the premium subscription tier, a light/dark theming option,
and the test suite and
deployment pipeline. As before, the architecture and technology stack of
Section~\ref{chap:requirements} are not repeated; this release
instantiates them for the transactional, intelligence and delivery
features.

\section{Sprint 3 --- Orders, Payment \& Administration}

\subsection{Sprint Objective}

Sprint~3 makes the marketplace transactable and governable. By the end of
the sprint a customer can build a cart, check out across several stores
in a single order, and pay securely through Stripe, receiving a
confirmation email; a seller can see the orders that include their
products; every signed-in user can manage their own account --- viewing
and editing their profile, changing their password and reviewing their
purchase history --- and an administrator can moderate stores and
products, suspend or delete a store, curate featured content and manage
the category tree.
The payment flow is the centrepiece: amounts are computed server-side,
charged through a single Stripe~\cite{stripe} PaymentIntent, and confirmed
asynchronously by a signature-verified webhook.

\subsection{Stripe Payment Design}

Payment is the most security-sensitive flow of the platform, and its
design follows a single principle: \textbf{the client never dictates what
it is charged}. When the customer checks out, the backend reserves stock,
creates the pending order(s) for the cart, and computes the grand total
from the server-side prices. Stripe accounts cannot settle in Tunisian
dinar, so the dinar total is converted to United States dollar minor
units through a configurable rate (\texttt{TND\_TO\_USD\_RATE}) while the
interface continues to display dinar. A single PaymentIntent is then
opened for the whole cart --- even when it spans several stores --- with
an \textbf{idempotency key} derived from the order identifiers, so that a
retried request never creates a duplicate charge. The PaymentIntent
identifier is stamped on the order(s) for later reconciliation.
Listing~\ref{lst:paymentintent} shows the core of this server-side
charge.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Server-side PaymentIntent creation (amount derived on the server).},label={lst:paymentintent}]
const intent = await stripe.paymentIntents.create(
  {
    // amount is computed server-side and converted TND -> USD minor units
    amount: tndToStripeUsdMinor(grandTotal),
    currency: 'usd',
    metadata: {
      orderIds: orderIds.join(','),
      displayCurrency: 'TND',
      displayAmount: grandTotal.toFixed(3),
    },
  },
  { idempotencyKey: `checkout-${orderIds.join('-')}` },
);
await orderService.setPaymentIntent(orderIds, intent.id);
\end{lstlisting}

Confirmation is asynchronous. Stripe calls an \textbf{unauthenticated}
\texttt{POST /payments/webhook} endpoint --- it is deliberately public,
because the caller is Stripe rather than a logged-in user. Trust is
established not by a session but by \textbf{verifying the Stripe
signature against the raw request body}; an unverifiable payload is
rejected. On a \texttt{payment\_intent.succeeded} event the order(s) are
marked \textsc{paid} and \textsc{confirmed} and the confirmation email is
sent; on a failed or canceled event the reserved stock is released. The
handler is idempotent, so a duplicated webhook is harmless. Finally, a
periodic cleanup sweep reconciles checkouts left unpaid past a staleness
window: it retrieves each PaymentIntent from Stripe and either settles a
paid-but-unacknowledged order or releases the stock of an abandoned one,
so a lost webhook can never strand stock or a payment.

\section{Sprint 4 --- AI Verification \& Premium Tier}

\subsection{Sprint Objective}

Sprint~4 adds the intelligence and monetisation layers. Every new store
and product is screened automatically by an AI verification pipeline
before it becomes visible; suspicious content is flagged for the
administrator, who remains the final authority. Administrators gain a
natural-language database chat assistant for diagnostics, and sellers
--- and only sellers --- can take out a \emph{recurring monthly} premium
subscription through Stripe Checkout, managed afterwards through the Stripe
Customer Portal, that unlocks the full customisation set and multiple
stores; this is a new payment mechanism, distinct from the one-time charge
of Sprint~3. The AI
capabilities are delivered through three independent n8n workflows
invoked by the backend over HTTP. Finally, the sprint rounds out the user
experience with an appearance option: a light and a dimmed dark theme,
chosen explicitly or following the operating-system preference and
applied consistently across the application chrome through CSS design
tokens.

moderates it against the marketplace rules with a vision model, and posts
the aggregated verdict back to the callback. The \textbf{database chat}
workflow (Figure~\ref{fig:n8n-chat}) wraps an AI agent --- with a chat
model, conversation memory and a SQL execution tool --- that answers an
administrator's natural-language question by querying the database. The
\textbf{storefront-redesign} workflow (Figure~\ref{fig:n8n-redesign}),
already detailed in Sprint~2, builds a prompt, calls the language model,
and parses and sanitises the returned customisation object.

\subsection{Premium Tier}

Unlike the one-time checkout of Sprint~3, the premium tier is a
\emph{recurring monthly subscription}, and it therefore introduces two
Stripe mechanisms beyond that sprint's single PaymentIntent:
\textbf{Stripe Checkout in subscription mode} for sign-up and the
\textbf{Stripe Customer Portal} for self-service management. A seller
starts the flow from the upgrade page; the backend creates --- or reuses
--- a Stripe \emph{customer} for the account, stamps its identifier on
\texttt{stripeCustomerId}, and opens a hosted Checkout session in
subscription mode for the configured monthly price. Everything afterwards
--- cancelling, updating the card, downloading invoices --- is delegated
to the hosted Customer Portal rather than rebuilt in-house.

The tier is \textbf{sellers-only}: the \texttt{premium/checkout} endpoint
is role-gated to the seller role, a non-seller is routed instead to the
become-a-seller flow, and administrators keep a \textbf{manual grant} comp
path that marks an account premium without any Stripe subscription.

Entitlement is \textbf{webhook-driven}. The subscription lifecycle events
--- \texttt{customer.subscription.created}, \texttt{...updated} and
\texttt{...deleted} --- arrive through the \emph{same} signature-verified
\texttt{/payments/webhook} introduced in Sprint~3. A created or updated
subscription sets \texttt{isPremium} and stamps \texttt{premiumExpiresAt}
with the current period end, so each successful renewal simply advances
the expiry; a scheduled cancellation is recorded in
\texttt{cancelAtPeriodEnd} without revoking access, so premium is kept
until the paid period actually ends; a deleted subscription revokes
premium and detaches the subscription id. To carry this, the
\texttt{User} entity gains \texttt{stripeCustomerId},
\texttt{stripeSubscriptionId} and \texttt{cancelAtPeriodEnd} alongside the
existing \texttt{isPremium} and \texttt{premiumExpiresAt}
(Figure~\ref{fig:class-release2}); the premium-status read returned to the
client now also exposes \texttt{cancelAtPeriodEnd} and a
\texttt{manageable} flag that tells a real subscriber --- who can open the
portal --- apart from an administrator comp grant.

Premium unlocks the full storefront customisation set --- free-form
colours, all fonts and radii, banner controls and the rest --- and the
ability to operate several stores. As before, the design deliberately
\emph{preserves} a seller's premium customisation values after expiry: the
stored design remains visible to buyers and only the editing of premium
fields is locked, so a lapsed subscription never degrades a storefront
that is already live (US27). Figure~\ref{fig:seq-premium-subscription}
traces the subscription exchange from checkout through the webhook-driven
entitlement update.

\section{Tests \& Validation}
\label{sec:tests}

Quality is enforced by an automated suite of \textbf{504 tests} that runs
on every push and gates every deployment.

\subsection{Testing Strategy and Tooling}

The backend is tested with \textbf{Jest~30}~\cite{jest} and \texttt{ts-jest}, which
transforms TypeScript on the fly and runs every co-located
\texttt{*.spec.ts} file; collaborators such as the Prisma service, the
notification service, the SMTP mailer and the authentication guards are
replaced with deterministic mocks. The frontend is tested with
\textbf{Vitest}~\cite{vitest} and React Testing Library with \texttt{jest-dom}, which
mount components, query the DOM by accessible role and text, and assert
on the rendered output. Good tests here verify observable behaviour ---
the result of a service call, the response of an endpoint, the rendered
state of a component --- rather than internal implementation details, so
they survive refactoring.

\subsection{Test Pyramid}

The suite follows the classical pyramid: a wide base of fast pure-logic
and frontend tests, a thicker middle of service-level tests that run the
real service code against a mocked Prisma client, and a narrow top of
integration tests that exercise the wired-up HTTP stack through
supertest and the global deny-by-default guard.
Figure~\ref{fig:test-pyramid} shows how the tests distribute across these
layers.

\subsection{Backend Unit and Integration Tests}

Backend unit tests inject an in-memory mock of the Prisma service into
the real service under test through the NestJS \texttt{TestingModule},
which lets each test drive arbitrary return values and assert on the
exact arguments passed to Prisma --- validating business rules such as
ownership enforcement, the server-side payment-amount derivation and the
stale-checkout reconciliation. Integration tests boot the Nest
application with its validation pipe and global guard and drive real
controllers through supertest, confirming that the deny-by-default
authorisation wiring admits exactly the intended routes.

\subsection{Frontend Tests}

The frontend tests cover the Zustand stores, the custom hooks and the
React components --- including the theme switch and the centralised
status-badge helper introduced with the light/dark theming --- mounting
them in a jsdom environment and asserting on accessible, user-visible
output. Together with the backend suite they give the layered coverage
summarised in the pyramid above.

continuous-integration pipeline: every push runs linting, a build and the
full test run for the backend and the frontend, and a red suite blocks

\section{DevOps \& Deployment}

The platform is delivered as a set of containers, provisioned on
reproducible infrastructure and shipped by an automated pipeline. The
logical and physical architecture were established in
Section~\ref{chap:requirements}; this section details the realisation of
the delivery chain.

\subsection{Containerisation}

Each service ships as a Docker image. The backend uses a
\textbf{multi-stage} build (Listing~\ref{lst:dockerfile}): a builder
stage installs dependencies, generates the Prisma client and compiles
the application, then prunes development dependencies, and a slim
production stage copies only the build artefacts and runs as the
unprivileged \texttt{node} user, applying database migrations on start.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Backend multi-stage Dockerfile (excerpt).},label={lst:dockerfile}]
# build stage
FROM node:20-alpine AS builder
RUN corepack enable && corepack prepare pnpm@10 --activate
WORKDIR /app
COPY package.json pnpm-lock.yaml ./
RUN pnpm install --frozen-lockfile
COPY . .
RUN npx prisma generate && pnpm run build && pnpm prune --prod

# production stage
FROM node:20-alpine
WORKDIR /app
COPY --from=builder /app/node_modules ./node_modules
COPY --from=builder /app/dist ./dist
COPY --from=builder /app/prisma ./prisma
USER node
EXPOSE 3000
CMD ["sh", "-c", "npx prisma migrate deploy && node dist/src/main"]
\end{lstlisting}

The services are composed with Docker Compose: a MariaDB database with a
health check, the backend, the frontend, an n8n engine (with a one-shot
initialiser that imports the version-controlled workflows), and a
\textbf{Caddy}~\cite{caddy} reverse proxy that terminates TLS with automatically
renewed Let's Encrypt certificates and adds the HTTP security headers.
Listing~\ref{lst:compose} shows the backend and proxy service blocks.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Docker Compose --- backend and reverse-proxy services (excerpt).},label={lst:compose}]
services:
  backend:
    image: registry/marketplace-backend:${IMAGE_TAG:-latest}
    restart: always
    depends_on:
      db:
        condition: service_healthy
    environment:
      DB_HOST: db
      NODE_ENV: production
      STRIPE_SECRET_KEY: ${STRIPE_SECRET_KEY}
      STRIPE_WEBHOOK_SECRET: ${STRIPE_WEBHOOK_SECRET}
      N8N_VERIFY_WEBHOOK_URL: http://n8n:5678/webhook/ai-verify
    volumes:
      - uploads:/app/uploads

  caddy:
    image: caddy:2-alpine
    restart: always
    ports: ["80:80", "443:443"]
    volumes:
      - ./Caddyfile:/etc/caddy/Caddyfile:ro
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Infrastructure as Code}

The production environment is described declaratively with
\textbf{Terraform} using the DigitalOcean~\cite{digitalocean} provider, so the whole server
can be recreated identically with a single \texttt{terraform apply} and
every change is reviewable as a diff. Three resources define the
environment: a private \texttt{digitalocean\_vpc}, an
\texttt{ubuntu-24-04-x64} \texttt{digitalocean\_droplet} attached to it
with the operator's SSH key, and a firewall configured through the
droplet's \texttt{user\_data} cloud-init script. That script makes the
first boot zero-touch: it installs Docker Engine and the Compose plugin,
then configures UFW to \emph{deny all inbound by default} while allowing
only SSH, HTTP, HTTPS and the n8n webhook port. The provider also manages
the DNS records that point the domain at the droplet.

\subsection{CI/CD Pipeline}
\label{sec:cicd}

A \textbf{GitHub Actions}~\cite{githubactions} workflow turns a push to \texttt{main} into a
live deployment (Listing~\ref{lst:deploy}). It first runs the backend and
frontend test jobs; only if both pass does it provision the
infrastructure with Terraform, build and push the backend and frontend
Docker images to the registry, and finally deploy by copying the Compose
file to the droplet over SSH and bringing the stack up. Secrets are
injected from the CI secret store, so no credential lives in the
repository, and DNS is updated to the droplet's address automatically.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={GitHub Actions build-and-deploy pipeline (excerpt).},label={lst:deploy}]
name: Build, Push & Deploy
on:
  push:
    branches: [main]

jobs:
  test-backend:    # pnpm install, prisma generate, lint, build, jest
    runs-on: ubuntu-latest
  test-frontend:   # pnpm install, lint, build, vitest
    runs-on: ubuntu-latest
  provision:       # terraform init + apply (droplet, firewall, DNS)
    runs-on: ubuntu-latest
  build-backend:   # docker build + push to the registry
    needs: [test-backend, test-frontend]
    runs-on: ubuntu-latest
  deploy:
    needs: [provision, build-backend, build-frontend]
    runs-on: ubuntu-latest
    steps:
      - name: Deploy over SSH
        run: ssh $HOST "docker compose pull && docker compose up -d"
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Production Environment}

In production the public entry point is the Caddy proxy, which terminates
HTTPS and forwards traffic to the frontend container; the frontend serves
the single-page application and proxies the API calls to the backend; the
backend talks to the MariaDB database and to the n8n engine that carries
the AI workflows. All services run under Docker Compose with restart
policies on a single DigitalOcean droplet, giving the simple,
reproducible and observable deployment targeted by the non-functional
requirements of Section~\ref{chap:requirements}.

\section*{Conclusion}

This chapter delivered Release~2 and, with it, a complete product.
Sprint~3 made the marketplace transactable --- a multi-store cart and
checkout, the server-authoritative Stripe payment flow confirmed by a
signature-verified webhook, the self-service account management that lets
every user edit their profile, change their password and review their
purchase history, and the administration tooling to moderate stores and
products, curate featured content and manage categories.
Sprint~4 added the intelligence and delivery layers --- the automated AI
verification pipeline with administrative override, the database chat
assistant, the premium tier built on the same payment machinery, a light
and dimmed-dark theming option, and the
testing and DevOps that bring the platform to production: a layered suite
of 504 tests, containerised services behind a TLS-terminating proxy,
infrastructure described with Terraform, and a GitHub Actions pipeline
that tests, builds and deploys on every push to \texttt{main}. The
marketplace planned in the first two chapters is now fully realised,
validated and deployable.
