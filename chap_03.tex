\chapter{Release~1: Foundations \& Core Marketplace}
\label{chap:release1}

\section*{Introduction}

This chapter opens the realisation of the platform. It covers the first
release, \textit{Foundations \& Core Marketplace}, which establishes the
identity layer, the seller-onboarding loop and the core selling
capabilities on which every later feature depends. The chapter begins
with a release overview --- a release-scoped use-case diagram and the
subset of the domain model touched by the release --- followed by the
release backlog. It then presents the two sprints of the release. The
present chapter details \textbf{Sprint~1 (Authentication \& Seller
Onboarding)}; \textbf{Sprint~2 (Store \& Product Management)} and the
chapter conclusion follow. Each sprint is described at a uniform depth:
its objective, its use-case diagram, an analysis-and-design section
(textual use-case descriptions, activity diagrams and sequence
diagrams), and a realisation section illustrating the delivered
interfaces.

\section{Release Overview}

Release~1 delivers the marketplace skeleton over two sprints. Sprint~1
builds authentication and the seller-onboarding loop, so that a visitor
can register, verify their email, sign in, and request the seller role
for an administrator to review. Sprint~2 builds store and product
management, so that an approved seller can open a store, customise its
storefront, populate a catalogue and run a store-wide sale, while
customers browse that catalogue by category. The architecture and the
technology stack that frame this realisation were established in
Section~\ref{chap:requirements} and are not repeated here; this release
instantiates them for the foundational features.

\subsection{Release Use-Case Diagram}

Figure~\ref{fig:uc-release1} scopes the use cases delivered by

\section{Sprint 1 --- Authentication \& Seller Onboarding}

\subsection{Sprint Objective}

Sprint~1 delivers the identity layer of the marketplace and the loop
that turns a customer into a seller. By the end of the sprint a visitor
can create an account secured by email verification, sign in and out
through database-backed sessions, and submit a seller application; an
administrator can review that application and, on approval, promote the
account to the seller role. Authentication is provided by the
\textbf{Better-Auth} library, integrated into the NestJS backend through
a thin adapter; it ships email/password credentials, email verification,
database-persisted sessions and cookie transport, so that no
cryptography is hand-written. Every protected route is gated by an
authentication guard and the \texttt{@Roles()} decorator, with
object-level ownership enforced in the service layer.

Figure~\ref{fig:uc-sprint1} details the sprint's use cases. Registration

\section{Sprint 2 --- Store \& Product Management}

\subsection{Sprint Objective}

Sprint~2 turns an approved seller into an active merchant. By the end of
the sprint a seller can create a store reachable at a human-readable
slug, customise its storefront across seven sections through a WYSIWYG
editor, populate a product catalogue with images and tags, and run a
store-wide sale; customers can browse that catalogue by category. The
storefront editor is built with \textbf{Craft.js}~\cite{craftjs}, where clicking any
section on the live canvas opens its settings panel, with undo/redo and
preset starting points. Several capabilities exposed here are
\textbf{premium-tiered}: the full customisation set, the operation of
multiple stores (US09) and the AI-assisted storefront redesign (US35) are
built on the storefront foundation in this sprint, but their premium
\emph{entitlement} is delivered by the premium tier in Release~2
(Chapter~\ref{chap:release2}); until then free accounts use the curated
subset and a single store.

\section*{Conclusion}

This chapter delivered Release~1, the foundations of the marketplace.
Sprint~1 established the identity layer --- registration with email
verification, database-backed sessions and role-based access --- together
with the seller-onboarding loop of application and administrative review.
Sprint~2 turned approved sellers into active merchants with store
creation, the seven-section storefront editor, AI-assisted redesign,
product management and store-wide sales, while opening catalogue browsing
to customers. The marketplace can now be populated with stores and
products, but it cannot yet transact. Release~2, presented in the next
chapter, adds the cart and checkout, the Stripe payment flow,
administration and moderation, automated AI verification, the premium
tier, and the testing and deployment that bring the platform to
production.
